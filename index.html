A Semester of Growth and Unfinished Work — Melchior Sanwidi · ENGL 101-30446 · Professor Atkins · 13 May 2026

Writing Across Two Worlds: A Semester of Growth and Unfinished Work

Writing has never been a neutral act for me. Long before I enrolled in ENGL 101, I had already experienced what it means to lose language — to sit in a classroom in Burkina Faso, surrounded by students who understood everything, and understand almost nothing. That experience of being lost in translation shaped how I approach communication, and it is part of why this semester's writing assignments felt personal in a way that academic work does not always feel. Reviewing the essays I have written — my narrative essay, "Lost in Translation," and my argument essay, "Institutional Protection Is a Myth" — I can trace a clear line of growth, even as I recognize the skills I have not yet mastered. I feel most competent in two course outcomes: writing essays that demonstrate facility with grammar and mechanics (EW) and developing unified essays that present central ideas supported by relevant personal experience, critical thought, and readings (EW). At the same time, I continue to struggle with formulating a thesis to anchor the development of a position appropriate to audience and purpose (CTRW) and developing my own ideas in relation to the words and ideas of others from instructor-selected readings (CTRW). This semester has shown me that growth as a writer is not the absence of struggle — it is learning to do something meaningful with it.

One of the most visible changes across my two essays is my improved control of grammar and mechanics at the sentence level. In my earliest drafts of "Lost in Translation," my sentences were often too long and ran into one another, blurring the emotional clarity that the narrative needed. I was trying to capture the disorientation of sitting in a French classroom as an American student, but fragmented or fused sentences were getting in the way of the story itself. Over the course of revising, I worked to give each sentence its own weight. The final draft shows this: moments of dialogue, such as when my classmate looks at me and says, "You didn't get that? He is literally talking about yesterday's lesson," are set apart cleanly, and the pacing of the narrative reflects a writer who is making deliberate choices about sentence rhythm rather than just transcribing memory. By the time I wrote "Institutional Protection Is a Myth," my sentences had become more complex and intentional. Paragraphs in that essay move between long analytical sentences and shorter declarative ones, which mirrors the argument's logic — building a case, then landing the point. The course outcome of writing essays that demonstrate facility with grammar and mechanics is not only about avoiding errors; it is about using sentence-level choices to serve meaning. My two essays, read side by side, show that I have moved in that direction.

A second area of genuine growth is my ability to develop unified essays that present central ideas supported by relevant personal experience, critical thought, and readings. "Lost in Translation" demonstrates this outcome most clearly at the personal level: every paragraph of that essay orbits the same central insight — that language is not just a tool for communication but a barrier that shapes identity and belonging. I did not state that idea at first; I learned it through the writing. The essay moves from confusion and isolation in the classroom, through the embarrassment of failing tests I had studied for, and finally to the relationships built with classmates who helped me catch up. None of those sections wanders. Each one adds to the same claim I arrive at in the conclusion: that identity is not just where one comes from, but how one adapts to a new space. My argument essay applies this same principle at a higher level of abstraction. The central claim — that institutional protection of minority rights is always contingent on institutional self-interest — is supported by the Supreme Court's refusal to hear the Space Traders case, by Professor Golightly's erasure despite his credentials, and by the convergence of government, media, church, and business around the same conclusion. Each paragraph pushes the same thesis forward. This unity did not come naturally; it came from revision, from instructor feedback, and from learning to ask at every paragraph: does this serve the central argument?

Despite this progress, I recognize that I have not yet mastered the outcome of formulating a thesis to anchor the development of a position appropriate to audience and purpose. This outcome asks for something more precise than an arguable claim: it asks for a thesis that is calibrated — one shaped by a writer's awareness of who is reading and why the argument matters to them. Looking at my argument essay honestly, I can see the gap. My thesis states that institutional protection is a myth and that The Space Traders reveals the true cost of depending on systems to defend marginalized people. This is a genuine argument, and it drives the essay forward. But the thesis does not yet show full rhetorical awareness. It does not signal to the reader — in this case, the English Department faculty, or a broader academic audience familiar with critical race theory — what specific lens I am using to enter the conversation, or why this argument has stakes beyond the text itself. Kimberlé Crenshaw's argument that institutional racism operates through ostensibly neutral policies is cited in my essay, but my thesis does not bring her framework into conversation with Bell's in a way that would strengthen my position for a scholarly reader. This is the work I need to do: to write thesis statements that are not just arguable but rhetorically situated — ones that show I understand my audience and have chosen my claim with that audience in mind.

The outcome I feel furthest from mastering is developing my own ideas in relation to the words and ideas of others from instructor-selected readings. This outcome requires a kind of intellectual conversation — one in which the writer's thinking is genuinely shaped and extended by the sources, not merely supported by them. In "Institutional Protection Is a Myth," I use two sources: Derrick Bell's "The Space Traders" and Kimberlé Crenshaw's "Mapping the Margins." I quote Bell three times in the essay, and each quotation is followed by analysis. However, when I look at what that analysis actually does, I notice that it tends to confirm what Bell has already said rather than extend or complicate it. Crenshaw appears in only one paragraph, and she is treated more as a supporting authority than as a thinker whose ideas I am putting into dialogue with Bell's. The gap between summary and analysis is one I can see in my own work, and closing it will require me to ask, at every source I use, not just "what does this say?" but "what does this mean for my argument, and how does it change what I think?"

The portfolio I am submitting is not a record of perfection. It is a record of a writer learning, in real time, what writing at the college level actually demands. The narrative essay gave me permission to bring my own experience into the classroom — to treat what happened to me in Burkina Faso not just as a memory but as an argument about language, identity, and belonging. The argument essay pushed me into a harder kind of thinking: analyzing a text through a theoretical lens, engaging with sources, building a case for a reader who does not know me. In both essays, I made real progress. But the two CTRW outcomes I have identified — thesis development for a specific audience and genuine intellectual engagement with sources — are not weaknesses I can leave behind. As a Business Analytics major, I know that upper-level coursework in business communications, report writing, and policy analysis will demand exactly these skills: the ability to frame an argument for a specific decision-making audience and to put outside research into meaningful conversation with my own analysis. The writing I did this semester is the foundation. The work ahead is to build on it.
